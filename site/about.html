Stuck in “deciphering”

Classics should be read and it should be read in original languages, not in translations, which deliver only shadow of genuine art and thought. If you are not a professional classicist, you either have never been taught classical languages formally, or have been for very short time — one term or two; maybe you have studied some manuals and grammars by yourself. Having went through such learning reading classics is difficult for you. Say, you would like to read your favorite classics work (some book of Homer, Plato, Vergil, Livy or some other text). In order to understand any sentence you need to do explicit morphological parsing, read grammatical notes in commentary and look in the lexicon to find the gloss for every third word. It isn’t reading in the usual sense of the word—“deciphering” would be a more accurate term—and it’s not the kind of experience you want to have in your engagement with Greek and Latin. Even if you persist and keep on “deciphering” book after book, you may find out that although you have become more efficient in it, genuine reading is nowhere in sight. Untill you rely on explicit knowledge of morphology, syntax, and vocabulary to piece together the meaning of a sentence, you haven’t essentially improved your understanding of Greek or Latin—you’re simply struggling with your inability to comprehend the language at that level.

Scholars of the past were somehow able not only fluently read, but also write and speak in Latin and Greek, so why we should be stuck in “deciphering”? Certainly there should be (a) something wrong with our standard approach to classical languages and (b) a way for improvement.

What’s wrong with standard approach?

Standard approach to teaching languages (not only classical) is often called “grammar-translation” (GT) method. Ineffectiveness of courses based on this approach is obvious: often students after years of study cannot produce correct sentence in target language. This sometimes leads to harsh criticisms, e.g. that GT has nothing to do with language acquisition because by using GT students are taught not language, but about language. Although distinction might be useful, we are not warranted to draw such extreme conclusion. Course books and courses based on GT are useful for language learning, we only need to understand their role and limitations.

The role and limitations of standard course books and courses can be clearly seen in the light of the simple theory explaining language acquisition. Language proficiency involves two key components: (1) a mental representation (MR) of the language, and (2) skill in using it. MR refers to the implicit knowledge your brain draws on to understand and produce language—encompassing morphology, syntax, vocabulary, semantics, and more. How did you get your MR of the language you are fluent in? The answer is actually quite straightforward: you build MR of a language by understanding the messages conveyed in it. Each time your brain links the language’s form—what you hear or read—to its meaning, your MR expands. Your real skills in reading, speaking, and listening depend on MR, which are formed through input. Because this input must be understood in order to be processed, it is commonly referred to as comprehensible input (CI). One should process vast amount of CI to develop MR on which fluency in the language could be based. Read more about language acquisition here, here and here.

Our course books and courses are designed to introduce new language items in each unit or lesson, with every stage bringing in fresh topics. Their structure ensures that each unit presents something new—whether grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, or other aspects—following a linear progression. However, this linear format reveals a limitation: course books tend to focus on introducing language rather than deepening knowledge of it. Beyond a token review unit or test, learners receive little meaningful practice or reinforcement. What is often missing is the consistent revisiting, recycling, and revision that are essential for true language acquisition. The underlying assumption of most courses and course books is that once learners have “met” a form, it has been “covered,” and the class can move on. While effective at presenting new material in sequence, course books—by design—are weak at recycling content and therefore poor at building lasting depth of knowledge. They cannot provide such a amount of CI which is needed to build MR necessary as a base for fluency in language.

This means that we expect too much from course books and language courses. This especially can be said about course books of classical languages whose authors usually suggest that after the studying materials of the course book the student will be able to read Plato or Vergil. Because students were not exposed to the large amounts of comprehensible input required to develop MR of the language, they are unable to achieve genuine comprehension when reading. Without a solid MR of Greek or Latin, they are essentially attempting to read a language they do not actually know. In this respect course books and courses based on GT and those based on “natural approach” (as e.g. Hans H. Ørberg’s Lingua Latina per se illustrata) are essentially similar – neither provide amount comprehensible input necessary for developing adequate MR for reading classics.

The correcly understood role of the course book or language course can be only this: by introducing main features of morphology, syntax and basic vocabulary to prepare student for engaging with CI which comes from some other sources.

This understanding of language acquisition and of the role of course books and courses allows to propose some improvements.

First improvement: Morphology drills

Most basic task the course book or language course should achieve is introduction to basic features of morphology. The student should be able quickly, automatically and in essence subconsciously recognize morphological features of the words and to derive from them the syntactical features of the sentence.

Usually such automatism is not achieved and students take pains at morphologicaly parsing even quite simple sentences. But without this skill the student is not prepared to engage with CI except to process the most basic sentences.

As a remedy for poor mastering of morphology professor William C. Dowling recommends to copy by hand certain morphology tables as many times as needed until the knowledge of paradigms develops into automatism. ParsedClassics project provides morphology tables for Attic Greek, Demotic Greek and Latin in which you can type answers; the anwers will be marked as right or wrong by clicking the button.

Second improvement: Extensive reading

If you have mastered some quality course book (such as Donald J. Mastronarde’s Introduction to Attic Greek or Hardy Hansen's and Gerald M. Quinn’s Greek: Intensive course or Robert J. Henle’s Latin or Hans H. Ørberg’s Lingua Latina per se illustrata) and mastered syntax paradigms by Dowling method, you are prepared to make further step – to develop your MR of the language in respect of syntax, vocabulary, semantics etc. This can only be achieved by engaging with CI, that is by doing vast amount of reading and listening at the level of your comprehension. For ancient Greek and Latin engaging with CI means essencially reading a lot of adapted texts and then reading a lot of simple unadapted texts.

Consuming large quantities of highly comprehensible texts fluently and quickly (at least 150-200 words per minute or a little lower for beginning students) is called “extensive reading”. For learners outside an immersive environment, extensive reading may be the most effective—if not the only—way to develop a wide-ranging vocabulary (along with awareness of usage, register, collocations, and colligations), to practice real-time sentence processing, and to internalize what sounds natural in the language and what does not.

Deep reading

Suppose that the vast amount of extensive reading has beed done and you have developed the skill of fluent reading in Greek or Latin. Now what?

Being fluent reader of Greek or Latin you are able to engage in the reading which can be called “deep reading” or “intensive reading”. Deep reading offers a kind of literary pleasure and level of insight that extensive reading cannot provide. Ideally, it begins with a text whose surface meaning is understood after a first reading; from there, the process deepens the reader’s understanding and enjoyment by focusing on how the text achieves its effects.

ParsedClassics project aims at providing classical texts equipped with best copyright-free scholarly aids – lexicons, concordances, commentaries, grammars etc. – using which you can not only read, but read achieving deep understanding and interpretation of the text, i.e. to practice deep reading.
